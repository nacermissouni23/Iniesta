## What I do

Design and apply Expected Goals models to quantify chance quality:

- Core xG features: shot distance, angle to goal, shot type (header/volley/foot), body part, assist type (through-ball/cross/set-piece), situation (open play/corner/free kick/penalty)
- Predict expected goals per shot and per match
- Compare actual goals to xG to measure finishing efficiency (residual analysis)
- Calculate xG chain and xG build-up to credit creators in the build-up
- Convert xG to expected points (xP) for team performance evaluation
- Apply xG for scouting: identify undervalued players in weaker leagues
- Use xG predictively: stronger predictor of future results than actual goals

## Key findings from The Expected Goals Philosophy

- xG is the most predictive single stat in football — better than actual goals for forecasting
- Even elite finishers (Ronaldo, Messi) barely outperform their xG consistently; their volume of chances is what sets them apart
- Brentford FC used xG to build a promotion-winning squad on a fraction of rivals' budgets
- Professional gamblers turned over hundreds of millions using xG models before clubs caught up
- xG has limitations: doesn't measure defensive pressure well, can't capture psychological factors, varies by model provider
- xG chain metrics better capture creative midfielders' contributions than goals or assists alone

## When to use me

Use this skill when building an xG model for historical data (estimating expected output for Zidane's era where xG doesn't exist), evaluating finishing efficiency, or comparing players across different tactical systems and eras.
